Reading 1:
How to Crit

Original article

1. What are some challenges you face when giving a critique?

One of the challenge that I face is not able to explain my work properly during critique. As someone who is nervous to speak about their work (or speak up in class in general), I often end up not being able to explain things clearly. This often cause some misunderstanding and affects the feedback I get. Another challenge that I face is not knowing which feedback to follow/not follow. But I often asked myself if the feedback is related to my concept in order to decide.

When it comes to giving feedback, the biggest challenge for me is not knowing what feedback to give to my peers. So often times, the feedback I give would usually end up as a compliment rather than a feedback. But through reading the article, I find that I can start by telling them what the work means in my perspective, and sometimes that may help them open up to an idea that they haven't thought of.

2. How do you handle a critique that you disagree with?

Most of the time during the live critique, I am too focused on listening and taking notes of what my peers are saying so I haven't really thought of whether or not I disagree with a feedback. But after the critique, when I am looking at the notes, I ask myself if the feedback would help reinforce my concept.

There are also times where I would disagree with a feedback during the live critique. I believe it is important to acknowledge the feedback whether I agree or disagree, so I would first thank them for their feedback, and then I would tell them my opinion. But I feel like most of the time I might have misunderstood their feedback, or wasn't able to see something that they saw, so I would usually restate my initial concept in order to remind them in case they forgot, and would ask them how their feedback relates to the concept.

Sometimes for the feedbacks that I disagree with, there might still be some parts that I can take from it and incorporate it with other feedbacks to generate another feedback.

3. Why do you think it’s important to give and receive critiques?

It is important to receive critiques because it is what makes our work better. Often times, we become emotionally attached to an idea very easily, which hinders the ability for our work to improve. There are many times where others may offer ideas that we haven't think of. I think through listening to other people's feedbacks, we can learn about their way of thinking, which not only can help us learn how to critique our own work, but also help us generate more creative ideas. As the article said "A lot of art and design education is about trying stuff until you figure out what works (How To Crit)", which is something that I definitely need to work on. At the same time, listening to other people presenting their work can help us improve our presentation of our work as well. The same reason applies to why it is important to give critiques.

Reading 2:
What Screens Want

Original article

1. Where do you stand with the two ideological camps: flat and skeuo? For example, do you think one is "better" than the other or do you strongly believe in one or the other.

In the article, Frank mentioned that those that are strongly believing in one or the other, are basing their opinions on aesthetics. I think we should look at it base on user experience.

Even though skeuomorphic design is able to replicate the analogue version of the element, I feel like sometimes having all those extra details would make the user hard to recognize right away what that element is.

Flat design, on the other hand, because it has no depth, it is hard to differentiate what stands out. Especially when it comes to elements like buttons, users might not exactly know that it is a button. Because it is flat and blends along with other elements.

That's why I think having a combination of both skeuo and flat is better. Using the button as an example, if there is a drop shadow underneath the flat button, the button is floating and it stands out from the other elements. At the same time, the shadow gives users a sense that it is something they can press, and when they press it, the shadow becomes darker, which indicates that the button got pushed down. So that can be like a feedback for the user.

Note: through searching online, I believe there's actually a term for the combination of the two: Material design (by Google).

2. What is a zoopraxiscope and how does it relate to web and interaction design? Find another example from filmmaking or another medium that has inspired digital design.

A zoopraxiscope is a device that projects motion by shining light onto a disc, which are composed of a series of photographs. The spinning disc switches the photographs at high speeds, which animates the object (like a flip book). It is similar to modern day GIFs, which are made from series of frames/images as well, and it loops.

Another medium that has inspired digital design would be a device that is similar to the zoopraxiscope - the old movie projectors (maybe the zoopraxiscope is what inspired the movie projector?). The navigation of a webpage (the scrolling part) is very similar to the concept of the film projector. The long webpage document is like the long strip of film. What we see on the browser viewport is like the projected image. The scrollbar is like the shuttle (a mechanism that hooks to the holes on the sides of the film, to pull the film down).

3. Do you agree with that screens want "flux"? Explain why you do or do not.

To be honest, I am not exactly sure what Frank meant by screens want flux. I interpreted flux, or change as like movement. Maybe flux is more general?

Based on my interpretation, I agree with Frank. I think screens are neutral, they aren't designed to be exactly like analogue. They can be permanent like analogue (like a pdf document that doesn't change), but they are usually temporary (like movement/animation or other customizations that we see in most websites and software today). But these temporary features can do more than analogue. Like the example that Frank talked about in low flux - the ability to filter your spreadsheet on screen. These changes can also contribute to functionality, like using subtle animations to give feedback to the user after an interaction (as Frank described in medium flux). While screen is not concrete and tangible like analogue, but they try to compensate that with interactivity and change.

Reading 3:
Touring Complete User

Original article

1. What do you think is Lialina's opinion of the word "user"?

Lialina believes the word "user" should not be made invisible by replacing with the general term "people". She mentioned that "being a user is the last reminder that there is, whether visible or not, a computer, a programmed system you use" (Turing Complete User). Using the word "people" also blurs the boundary between developers and users, which may cause the users to lose their data and privacy rights. In general, Lialina isn't really fond of the idea of the Invisible User and Invisible Computer, because it takes away the customization and ultimately, control/freedom of the users.

Lialina also believes that "user" is not a bad term. To programmers and hackers, the "user" is the best role when it comes to testing a piece of software/hardware. Furthermore, based on Vanevar Bush's text that she quoted, it seems like the user is even more important that the piece of hardware itself.

2. What is Lialina's ideal internet user? Provide some of the qualities that she outlines.

Lialina's ideal internet user is the General Purpose Users. The General Purpose Users are users who basically have many ways to achieve one task, instead of just following the way they are designed to be done by developers. This way, users are more engaged with the hardware and software, which aligns with what the web 2.0 is designed for, which encourages people to contribute, and establishing personal connections between the builder and the consumer of the app. These connections can be established by as Liliana called it "filling the gaps", which I believe refers to filling up the features that were not implemented by the builders.

Lialina also mentioned how users were often thought of as being too busy, and the computer was a tool for them to perform their repetitive thoughts so they can save their brain power for something else. However, when all technology are designed based on this notion, every task become automated to the point where the user is not busy anymore, and it eventually robs them away from their creative freedom.

The General Purpose Users are also known as Universal or Turing Complete Users, because like Alan Turing's Universal Machine, where it can solve any logical problem given enough time and memory, the General Purpose Users can achieve anything as long as there is enough time.

3. From your own experiences interacting on the web—what is an example where there are "gaps that can be filled by users"?

From my own experience with the web, an example of "filling the gaps" can be for OneNote, there isn't a built in calendar/agenda function, so I drew my own calendars using the table function. I can use other calendar apps, but because they all required making an account and being someone who forgets things easily, I choose not to.

Another example can be for Instagram. I really hope they can implement a feature where users can filter out what type of content appear on their homepage. I often wanted to see work from the designers I followed, but I ended up getting distracted by a photo of food when I scrolled down to see the next post. As a way to get around that, I created multiple Instagram accounts, one for design, and the other for personal. But as I become interested in other content, I might have to create many Instagram accounts.

Reading 4:
My website is a shifting house next to a river of knowledge

Original article

1. Why does Laurel Schwulst advocate for individual websites over social media? Do you agree with her? Please explain your answer.

Schwulst advocate for individual websites over social media because the idea of having the web controlled by a few large dominating corporations isn't what the web really is about. She said the web is "an interconnected web of individual nodes supporting a vast network" (My website is a shifting house next to a river of knowledge. What could yours be?). We need to expand the web further so that it truly represents what its metaphor refers to, and so that it lives up to its name of the "world wide web". Schwulst also talked about how large social media sites like Facebook, Instagram, Snapchat and Pinterest care about profit first before its users' needs.

I agree with Schwulst. Even though we have accounts in social media, but at the end of the day, we don't actually have full control over them. But when we create our own websites, we have full control. We can customize how we exactly envision our website to be. In a way, the web is like the digital version of our world if we have individual websites. Those individual website(s) represent our individual self, our own thoughts and imaginations. This is what Tim Berners Lee wanted, for the web to "reflect our hopes and fulfil our dreams" (My website is a shifting house next to a river of knowledge. What could yours be?). Having everyone's profiles fall under a template in social media really takes away the uniqueness in ourselves.

But considering the web right now, where a lot of its users come from social media platforms, it might be very hard to reshape the web into what Schwulst is advocating for. Take Twitter for example, many new users would most likely prefer to tweet on Twitter because there is already a huge established audience in Twitter. If they were to tweet on their own website, users might not be able to find it, so there isn't that much engagement.

2. In this article, Schwulst outlines several metaphors defining the behaviour of websites. Write a paragraph describing your own metaphor for a website. Put that text into its own simple website designed to extend that comparison. Upload it to your github server and post the link here.

I think a website is like a pebble in the ocean. Each pebble is unique as they vary in sizes and form. Each website is unique as well. They have different sizes, which are measured in pages, and they have different forms or structures. Like most pebbles, websites don't get discovered easily. The only way to find them is through links from other websites, and that usually comes from coincidence. Just like how the ocean may decide to one day grab that piece of pebble and push it to the shore, allowing someone to discover it. There are many websites in the web, just like many pebbles in the ocean. And most of the time, the waves of the ocean pushes multiple pebbles to the shore, just like how the search engine displays multiple websites at the same time. It is up to the person on the shore, or the user online to decide which pebble, or website to pick. But that doesn't mean the person can only pick one pebble. They can visit all the websites.

Reading 5:
Poetry of Tools

Original article

1. In the opening paragraph of her post, Seu asks you, the reader to join her to 'defamiliarize our tools.' What do you think she means by that? And how do you think that action relates to her use of the word poetry?

In the opening paragraph, Seu writes "Most use their devices in an automated way, without reflecting on their gestures" (The Poetry of Tools). I think Seu is referring about how especially in today's age where technology has advanced a lot (to the point where it seems "flawless"), a lot of users don't really pay much attention to how the technology actually works, and how their gestures impact that piece of technology. So I think when Seu is telling us to join her to "defamiliarize our tools", she is telling us to think consciously about the technology - to think more and engage deeply about it, rather than just scrolling and clicking to get from one goal to another.

Seu also gave new identities to the mundane screen and the cursor that we see everyday. She described how the audience is the person on the "other side of this glass screen" (The Poetry of Tools), and the mouse is her "avatar". This is similar to poetry, where a lot of the times poets would explain the ordinary in a very unordinary and new perspective, which allows us to see the world in a different point of view.

2. Seu outlines 6 categories within her taxonomy of tool poetics (Slow Growth & Slow Death, Early Works, Performing Data, Removing Function from Form, Production of Errors, & Regeneration). Choose at least one of these categories and define it in your own words. Now find a website or online interface* not mentioned in her post that you feel operates within the chosen definition. Explain why.

For "Slow Growth & Slow Death", I interpreted it as Seu implying that like nature and human beings, the web is a living thing as well, since it can slowly grow and eventually die.

Seu's example of Penelope Umrico's All the Catalogs (A-Z) reminds me of the Wayback Machine, which can be can be an example of "Slow Growth & Slow Death". It shows historic snapshots of websites. Through looking back in time, you can definitely identify the slow growth of the website from its change in features, and the interface. Often times, some webpage of the website either gets deleted, or the whole website gets replaced by another website (that brought the domain). I think this represents the "death" of the website. I feel like the state of the website can also reflect the creator themselves behind the website. Take a company website for example, the change in interface over periods of time can represent the company growth slowly, and the "death" of the website can represent the disband/end of the company.
